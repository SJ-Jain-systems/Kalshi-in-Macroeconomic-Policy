# 4. Institutional Adoption Pathway

*Status: new section — the FEDS paper never asks how the Fed or Treasury
could actually use this data in a decision process. This is a policy/
institutional-design question, not a data question.*

## What the FEDS paper already signals, without saying so explicitly

- The paper is itself published in the Fed Board's own **FEDS working paper
  series**, co-authored by a Federal Reserve Board economist. That's
  already a (soft, staff-level) institutional foothold — worth naming
  explicitly as evidence the pathway has *started*, informally.
- Footnote/closing paragraph (p.4): the authors "intend (subject to
  approval) to make the distributional daily-level data publicly available
  via an interactive website at EconFutures.com... to facilitate further
  research and enable policymakers to easily monitor shifts in investors'
  beliefs in real-time." That is literally a proposed dashboard — this
  section should treat it as the baseline proposal to evaluate and extend,
  not ignore.

## Research questions

1. **Does the Fed already cite market-based measures like this?** Check:
   - FOMC minutes and Chair press conference transcripts for references to
     OIS-implied paths, fed funds futures-implied probabilities, or TIPS
     breakeven inflation compensation.
   - The Monetary Policy Report and Tealbook/Bluebook (to the extent
     public/declassified) for use of market-based measures as one input
     among several.
   - Beige Book: is it purely qualitative/anecdotal, or does it ever cite
     quantitative market data? (My prior: purely qualitative — verify.)
   - Regional Fed president speeches for informal references to "market
     pricing" or "futures markets" implying policy expectations — this is
     probably the most common *current* form of market-based citation, and
     sets the bar Kalshi would need to clear to get similar treatment.
2. **What would "formal incorporation" actually look like, concretely?**
   Lay out a spectrum from lowest to highest institutional commitment:
   - **(a) Internal staff monitoring tool** — analysts watch a Kalshi
     dashboard the way they already watch fed funds futures, informally,
     with no public citation. Lowest bar; arguably already happening given
     this FEDS paper exists.
   - **(b) Public staff research citation** — more FEDS/Fed working papers
     and staff notes cite Kalshi-implied probabilities, without it becoming
     an official communications input. Medium-low bar.
   - **(c) Communications-level citation** — a Fed governor or Chair
     references "prediction markets are currently pricing X% odds of..." in
     a speech or press conference, the way officials sometimes reference
     fed funds futures today. Medium-high bar; raises the optics questions
     in Section 5.
   - **(d) Standing public data feed / dashboard** — an official or
     official-adjacent (e.g., NY Fed markets desk-style) real-time page,
     analogous to how the NY Fed publishes SOFR and repo data. Highest
     bar, requires the safeguards from Section 6.
3. **Precedent from adjacent agencies.** Does the Treasury, BLS, or CBO cite
   any prediction-market or betting-market data in any formal capacity
   currently? (Reasonable prior: no — this would strengthen the "novel
   territory" framing.)

## Deliverable for this section

A short comparison table: instrument (OIS / TIPS breakevens / fed funds
futures / Kalshi) × current level of Fed institutional use (per the
(a)–(d) spectrum above), sourced to specific FOMC minutes, speeches, or
publications rather than assumption.
